% 1.1  HEDGING UNDER NON-UNIQUENESS OF MARTINGALE MEASURES

\subsection{Hedging Under Non-Uniqueness of Martingale Measures}
\label{subsec:hedging_motivation}

The modern theory of derivative pricing rests on the principle that, in a
frictionless market with continuous trading, the price of a contingent
claim is determined by an arbitrage argument rather than by the
preferences of individual agents.  In the setting of
\citet{BlackScholes1973} and \citet{Merton1973}, a single risky asset
follows geometric Brownian motion with constant volatility~$\sigma$, and
every European option payoff can be exactly replicated by a dynamically
adjusted portfolio.  The cost of this replicating portfolio is unique: it
equals the discounted expectation of the payoff under the sole probability
measure that makes discounted prices into martingales.

The mathematical foundation is provided by the fundamental theorems of
asset pricing.  The first theorem \citep{DelbaenSchachermayer1994} states
that a market satisfies \text{no free lunch with vanishing risk} (NFLVR)
if and only if there exists at least one probability measure
$\mathbb{Q} \sim \mathbb{P}$ under which discounted asset prices are local
martingales.  The set of all such \text{equivalent local martingale
measures} is denoted~$\mathcal{M}(X)$.  Financially, each measure
$\mathbb{Q} \in \mathcal{M}(X)$ represents one consistent way of
assigning prices to uncertain future payoffs: it re-weights the likelihood
of different market scenarios so that, on average, discounted asset prices
neither rise nor fall.  The second theorem
\citep{HarrisonPliska1981, HarrisonKreps1979} characterises market
completeness: every contingent claim can be replicated if and only if
$|\mathcal{M}(X)| = 1$.  When a unique measure exists, every derivative
has exactly one arbitrage-free price, and a trading strategy that
perfectly reproduces the payoff can be constructed.

The structural issue that motivates this thesis is the failure of this
uniqueness.  In practice, volatility evolves randomly over time
\citep{Heston1993}, asset prices exhibit jumps \citep{Merton1976}, and
transaction costs prevent continuous rebalancing
\citep{LelandReplication1985}.  In multi-asset settings, portfolios may be
exposed to correlated risk factors that are not individually tradable.
Each of these features introduces sources of randomness that cannot be
offset by the available instruments, rendering the market
\text{incomplete}: $|\mathcal{M}(X)| > 1$.  Intuitively, an incomplete
market is one in which there are more sources of risk than there are
instruments to trade them away, so that perfect replication of a general
payoff is impossible.

When the market is incomplete, different measures in~$\mathcal{M}(X)$
assign different prices to the same non-replicable claim.  Rather than a
unique price, one obtains the arbitrage-free price interval
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:price_interval}
\Bigl[\,
  \inf_{\mathbb{Q} \in \mathcal{M}(X)} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[H],
  \;\;
  \sup_{\mathbb{Q} \in \mathcal{M}(X)} \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[H]
\,\Bigr].
\end{equation}
Any price within this interval is consistent with the absence of
arbitrage; any price outside it creates a free lunch
\citep{DelbaenSchachermayer1994}.  Financially, the interval represents
the range of prices at which neither the buyer nor the seller can extract a
risk-free profit: each measure~$\mathbb{Q}$ yields a different but
legitimate valuation, and the market provides no mechanism to select among
them.  The upper bound
$\pi(H) := \sup_{\mathbb{Q} \in \mathcal{M}(X)}
\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[H]$ has a direct financial interpretation grounded
in martingale duality: it equals the minimal initial capital required to
construct an admissible wealth process that dominates~$H$ almost surely at
maturity.  In other words, it is the cheapest amount of money a seller
must hold today to guarantee that, by trading in the available assets, the
portfolio will cover the payoff regardless of which scenario materialises.
This is the \text{super-hedging price}.  In a complete market, the
interval degenerates to a single point because only one pricing measure
exists; computationally, this means a single expectation suffices.  In an
incomplete market, computing the upper bound requires an optimisation over
the entire set~$\mathcal{M}(X)$, which is generally infinite-dimensional.

American options compound this difficulty through optimal stopping.  The
holder may exercise at any stopping time $\tau \le T$, and the seller's
hedge must remain adequate regardless of when exercise occurs.  The hedging
problem is therefore coupled to an optimal stopping problem, and the
minimal dominating price acquires a minimax structure:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:american_price_intro}
\pi(\xi)
\;:=\;
\sup_{\mathbb{Q} \in \mathcal{M}(X)}
\;\sup_{\tau \in \mathcal{T}_{[0,T]}}
\;\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[\xi_\tau],
\end{equation}
where $\xi := (\xi_t)_{t \in [0,T]}$ is the discounted reward process and
$\mathcal{T}_{[0,T]}$ is the set of stopping times valued in~$[0,T]$.
The nested supremum creates a two-player adversarial structure: the outer
supremum searches over all consistent pricing models (choosing the
worst-case measure), while the inner supremum searches over all possible
exercise times (choosing the worst-case exercise for the seller).  The
seller must be prepared for the worst combination of both.  In complete
markets, the outer supremum is trivial (there is only one measure) and the
problem reduces to the classical Snell envelope
\citep{Bensoussan1984, Karatzas1988}, a single optimal stopping problem.
In incomplete markets, both suprema are non-trivial: the seller faces
adversarial choice over both the pricing measure and the exercise policy,
making the problem substantially harder.  Computationally, this means that
any numerical method must handle the interaction of two coupled
optimisation layers, which rules out straightforward dynamic programming
unless the model has special structure.

The central object of this thesis is the \text{minimal dominating wealth
process} under non-unique martingale measures: the adapted process that
tracks this value dynamically through time, together with the trading
strategy that generates it.

% 1.2  MATHEMATICAL FORMULATION
% =========================================================================
\subsection{Mathematical Formulation of the Problem}
\label{subsec:math_formulation}

Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F},
\mathbb{F} := (\mathcal{F}_t)_{t \ge 0}, \mathbb{P})$ be a filtered
probability space satisfying the usual conditions of right-continuity and
completeness.  The filtration $\mathbb{F}$ represents the information
available to the hedger at each point in time: $\mathcal{F}_t$ contains
everything that has been observed up to and including time~$t$.  The space
supports $d := 2$ traded risky assets with discounted price process
$X := (X^1, X^2)$, assumed to be a locally bounded, c\`{a}dl\`{a}g
(right-continuous with left limits) semimartingale.  Let $\mathcal{M}(X)$
denote the set of all probability measures $\mathbb{Q} \sim \mathbb{P}$
under which~$X$ is a local martingale.  The market is assumed to be
incomplete: $|\mathcal{M}(X)| > 1$, reflecting the presence of
$m > d := 2$ independent sources of randomness.  In the models considered
in this thesis, there are two traded assets but three or more independent
Brownian motions driving the system, so that perfect hedging is impossible.

A \text{trading strategy} is a predictable, $X$-integrable process
$\varphi := (\varphi^1, \varphi^2)$ representing the number of units of
each asset held at each time.  Predictability means that the portfolio
position at time~$t$ is determined using only information available
strictly before~$t$: the hedger must decide how much of each asset to hold
\text{before} observing the next price movement.  Given initial
capital $v \ge 0$ and strategy~$\varphi$, the \text{wealth process} is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:wealth_process}
V_t
\;:=\; v \;+\; \int_0^t \varphi_s \, dX_s \;-\; C_t,
\qquad t \ge 0,
\end{equation}
where $\int_0^t \varphi_s \, dX_s$ represents the cumulative gains from
trade and $C := (C_t)_{t \ge 0}$ is an adapted, non-decreasing process
with $C_0 := 0$.  Financially, the integral $\int_0^t \varphi_s \, dX_s$
is the total profit or loss from holding the portfolio: it accumulates the
gains and losses from each small price movement, weighted by the number of
shares held.  The process~$C$ records the cumulative cost that arises
because the market does not offer enough instruments to track the
obligation perfectly.  When $C \equiv 0$, the strategy is self-financing:
changes in wealth are driven solely by changes in asset prices, and no
external cash injection is needed.  When $C$ is strictly increasing, the
hedger must absorb a running cost, reflecting the irreducible price of
operating in an incomplete market.  The strategy is \text{admissible} if
$V_t \ge 0$ for all $t \ge 0$ \citep{Kramkov1996}; this rules out
strategies that borrow indefinitely.

The American put option with strike~$K$ and maturity~$T$ on asset~$i$
generates a discounted reward process
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:american_reward}
\xi_t \;:=\; e^{-rt}\max(K - S^i_t,\, 0),
\qquad t \in [0, T].
\end{equation}
This is the payoff the holder would receive if exercising at time~$t$,
discounted back to time zero.  An admissible strategy with
$V_t \ge \xi_t$ for all $t \in [0,T]$ is a \text{dominating strategy}
for~$\xi$: the portfolio value always exceeds the option obligation, so
the seller can meet the claim no matter when the holder exercises.  The
\text{minimal dominating value process} is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:value_process}
\tilde{V}_t
\;:=\;
\esssup_{\mathbb{Q} \in \mathcal{M}(X)}
\;\esssup_{\tau \in \mathcal{T}_{[t,T]}}
\;\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[\xi_\tau \mid \mathcal{F}_t].
\end{equation}
The essential supremum ($\esssup$) is the measure-theoretic analogue of a
pointwise maximum: it selects the largest value across all pricing measures
and all stopping times, subject to the information available at time~$t$.
Intuitively, $\tilde{V}_t$ is the smallest amount of wealth that the
seller must hold at time~$t$ to guarantee coverage of the American option
from that point onward, accounting for the worst-case pricing model and
the worst-case exercise time.  At time zero, $\tilde{V}_0$ reduces to the
minimal dominating price~$\pi(\xi)$.

Under appropriate integrability conditions, the \text{optional
decomposition theorem} \citep{Kramkov1996} guarantees that~$\tilde{V}$
admits the representation
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:optional_decomp}
\tilde{V}_t
\;:=\; \tilde{V}_0
\;+\; \int_0^t \tilde{\varphi}_s \, dX_s
\;-\; \tilde{C}_t,
\qquad t \ge 0,
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\varphi}$ is a predictable, $X$-integrable process and
$\tilde{C}$ is an adapted, non-decreasing, c\`{a}dl\`{a}g process with
$\tilde{C}_0 := 0$.  This theorem is the cornerstone of the thesis and
merits careful interpretation.  It states three things.  \text{First},
the minimal dominating value can be decomposed into a starting capital
$\tilde{V}_0$, a gains-from-trade component
$\int_0^t \tilde{\varphi}_s \, dX_s$, and a cost
component~$\tilde{C}_t$.  \text{Second}, the trading
strategy~$\tilde{\varphi}$ is predictable, meaning it can be determined
before observing the current price movement.  \text{Third}, the cost
process~$\tilde{C}$ is only \text{adapted}: its value at time~$t$ may
depend on information revealed at~$t$ itself, not merely on information
available before~$t$.  This is why the decomposition is called
\text{optional} rather than \text{predictable}, in contrast to the
classical Doob--Meyer decomposition in which the increasing process is
predictable \citep{KaratzasShreve1991}.

Financially, the decomposition separates two fundamentally different
components of the hedging problem.  The integral
$\int_0^t \tilde{\varphi}_s \, dX_s$ captures the part of the value
process that \text{can} be generated by trading: it is what the hedger
earns through skilful portfolio management.  The process~$\tilde{C}_t$
captures the part that \text{cannot} be generated by trading: it is the
cumulative cost of operating in an incomplete market, the price paid for
the fact that the available instruments do not span all sources of risk.
In a complete market, $\tilde{C} \equiv 0$ and the decomposition reduces
to self-financing replication: the entire value process is generated by
trading alone.  In an incomplete market, $\tilde{C}$ is strictly
increasing and measures the irreducible cost of the gap between available
instruments and the risks to be managed.  The full statement and financial
interpretation are presented in Chapter~2.

The distinction between predictable and adapted is central to this thesis
and has a direct computational consequence.  The
integrand~$\tilde{\varphi}$ is predictable: the hedger's portfolio
position at time~$t$ depends only on information available strictly
before~$t$.  The cost process~$\tilde{C}$ is adapted but generally not
predictable: its value at time~$t$ may depend on information revealed
at~$t$ itself, such as a sudden volatility shock or an unexpected
correlation change.  A neural network that maps the current market state to
a portfolio position at each rebalancing date is, by construction, an
estimator of the predictable integrand~$\tilde{\varphi}$: it produces a
trading decision based on information available at the time the decision is
made.  The residual hedging error --- the gap between the trained portfolio
value and the option obligation --- captures the component of the value
process that the predictable strategy cannot reach.  This residual serves
as the empirical proxy for the adapted cost process~$\tilde{C}$.
Computationally, the trained neural network separates the hedging problem
into its learnable part (the strategy~$\tilde{\varphi}$, parameterised by
network weights) and its irreducible part (the cost~$\tilde{C}$, observed
in the residual).

The theorem is \text{existential}.  It guarantees the existence
of~$\tilde{\varphi}$ and~$\tilde{C}$ but provides no constructive
algorithm for computing either.  It tells us that an optimal predictable
strategy exists and that the remaining cost is adapted, but it does not
tell us what the strategy looks like in any specific model.  This gap
between the existence guarantee and the need for a computable solution
defines the problem addressed in this thesis: to approximate~$\tilde{\varphi}$
using established neural network methods, and to interpret the residual
hedging error as an empirical estimate of~$\tilde{C}$.

% 1.3  COMPUTATIONAL CHALLENGE AND RESEARCH GAP
% =========================================================================
\subsection{Computational Challenge and Research Gap}
\label{subsec:computational_challenge}

Computing the optional decomposition in concrete settings requires solving
high-dimensional, path-dependent optimisation problems for which no
closed-form solutions exist.  Classical PDE methods apply when the price
process is Markovian and low-dimensional, but scale poorly with the number
of state variables: in the two-asset Heston model considered here, the
state space is at least four-dimensional (two asset prices and two
variance processes), and grid-based methods become impractical
\citep{Glasserman2003}.  Moreover, PDE-based value functions depend on the
choice of pricing measure, and no single PDE characterises the minimal
dominating value process without additional structural information
about~$\mathcal{M}(X)$.  Regression-based Monte Carlo methods, notably the
Longstaff--Schwartz algorithm \citep{LongstaffSchwartz2001}, address
optimal stopping but do not directly yield hedging strategies and do not
account for model ambiguity in incomplete markets.

Deep learning methods offer alternatives that bypass these limitations.
Two fundamentally different approaches are relevant, distinguished by
whether they solve the problem \text{backward} or \text{forward} in
time.

The \text{deep BSDE solver} \citep{HanJentzenE2018} approximates the
backward stochastic differential equation (BSDE) that characterises the
value process.  Given a terminal condition (the option payoff), it
propagates the solution back through the time grid, parameterising the
initial value~$Y_0$ and the control process~$Z_{t_k}$ at each discrete
step with feedforward sub-networks.  The structural correspondence to the
optional decomposition is direct and important: the process~$Y$ maps to
the wealth~$V$, the control~$Z$ maps to the predictable
integrand~$\tilde{\varphi}$, and the driver of the backward equation
encodes the cost structure associated with~$\tilde{C}$.  The network is
therefore directly learning the object whose existence the optional
decomposition theorem guarantees.

By contrast, the \text{deep hedging} framework
\citep{BuehlerGononTeichmannWood2019} operates \text{forward} in time:
it parameterises the trading strategy directly as a neural network and
trains it end-to-end by minimising a risk functional applied to the
distribution of hedging errors over simulated paths.  Rather than
solving a backward equation, it searches directly over strategies to find
one whose terminal hedging error is small.  Within this forward framework,
two architectural choices are considered: a GRU recurrent network
\citep{Cho2014} that maintains an evolving hidden state updated at each
observation, and a feedforward network with \text{path signature
features} \citep{Horvath2021} that constructs a static, fixed-dimensional
mathematical summary of the path history.  A detailed review of these
methods and their extensions is deferred to Chapter~2.

The distinction between the backward and forward approaches has
consequences for how each architecture captures the predictable and adapted
components of the optional decomposition.  The deep BSDE solver directly
targets the backward equation whose solution encodes the predictable
integrand~$\tilde{\varphi}$.  The forward architectures optimise a risk
functional over the terminal hedging error, implicitly
learning~$\tilde{\varphi}$ through the gradient of the loss.  In both
cases, whatever the network cannot learn ends up in the residual, which
reflects the cost process~$\tilde{C}$.  Whether the GRU's recurrent
memory, which updates its hidden state with information revealed at each
time step, offers an advantage in capturing the adapted (non-predictable)
nature of~$\tilde{C}$ relative to the static signature encoding is an
empirical question that the thesis investigates.

Three specific gaps remain in the existing computational literature.
First, no systematic comparison of these architectures has been conducted
for American options under controlled incomplete-market models, where the
neural network output is explicitly interpreted as the estimator
of~$\tilde{\varphi}$ and the residual error as the empirical proxy
for~$\tilde{C}$.  The absence of such a comparison is not accidental; it
reflects the independent origins of the three methods.  Deep BSDE solvers
emerged from the numerical PDE and backward stochastic differential
equation community \citep{HanJentzenE2018, PardouxPeng1990}, where the
primary applications have been high-dimensional semilinear PDEs and
European-style claims.  Deep hedging originated in quantitative finance and
risk management \citep{BuehlerGononTeichmannWood2019}, with a focus on
transaction costs, market frictions, and regulatory constraints for
European portfolios.  Path signature methods entered computational finance
from the rough path theory literature \citep{Horvath2021, Lyons1998},
initially as feature extractors for volatility modelling rather than as
hedging architectures.  Each method has been developed, tested, and
validated within its own research community, against its own baselines, and
under its own experimental conventions.  As a result, reported performance
differences across studies may reflect differences in market models, loss
functions, hyperparameter budgets, or evaluation protocols rather than
genuine architectural capabilities.  Furthermore, American options under
incomplete markets compound two difficulties --- optimal stopping and model
ambiguity --- that most existing studies avoid by restricting attention to
European claims or to complete-market settings.  The combination of early
exercise, non-unique martingale measures, and the optional decomposition
perspective has therefore not served as a test bed for any of these
methods, let alone for a controlled comparison among them.

Comparing all three architectures under identical conditions is important
for three reasons.  First, the architectures encode fundamentally different
inductive biases about the structure of the hedging problem: the deep BSDE
solver imposes the backward equation structure and learns~$\tilde{\varphi}$
through the control variable~$Z$; the GRU learns a forward strategy with
recurrent memory that may capture path-dependent and adapted features; the
FNN with path signatures compresses the path history into a static
algebraic summary before mapping it to a decision.  These are not minor
implementation variants --- they represent distinct hypotheses about which
representation of market information leads to the best approximation of
the predictable integrand.  Second, without a unified experimental design,
it is impossible to determine whether observed differences in hedging
performance are due to the architecture itself or to confounding factors
such as the choice of loss function, the number of training paths, or the
hyperparameter search procedure.  Third, the optional decomposition
provides a principled theoretical lens through which to interpret each
architecture's output: the network approximates~$\tilde{\varphi}$, and the
residual reveals~$\tilde{C}$.  Comparing residuals across architectures
under the same market model yields empirical evidence on the extent to
which each method captures the hedgeable component and exposes the
irreducible cost of incompleteness.

Deep hedging studies have primarily addressed European claims under
transaction costs and market frictions; applications to American options
under model ambiguity within the optional decomposition framework are
absent.  Second, the distinct contributions of
\text{structural} incompleteness (dimensional mismatch between traded
assets and Brownian drivers, fixed at model specification) and
\text{dynamic} incompleteness (stochastic volatility, evolving randomly
through time) to the residual hedging cost have not been disentangled
empirically.  Third, the residual error of trained hedging models has not
been validated against the theoretical prediction that
$\tilde{C} \equiv 0$ in the complete-market limit and $\tilde{C} > 0$
otherwise.

\paragraph{Loss function and actuarial rationale.}
All three architectures are trained using a common asymmetric loss
function:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:loss}
\mathcal{L}(\theta)
\;:=\; \lambda_s \, \mathbb{E}\bigl[\max(\xi_\tau - V_T^\theta,\, 0)\bigr]
\;+\; \lambda_o \, \mathbb{E}\bigl[\max(V_T^\theta - \xi_\tau,\, 0)\bigr]
\;+\; \lambda_c \, \operatorname{CVaR}_\alpha\!\bigl(\max(\xi_\tau
      - V_T^\theta,\, 0)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $V_T^\theta$ is the terminal portfolio value under
strategy~$\varphi_\theta$ and $\operatorname{CVaR}_\alpha$ denotes
conditional value-at-risk at level~$\alpha$
\citep{RockafellarUryasev2000}.  The three terms encode distinct financial
objectives.

The first term, weighted by~$\lambda_s$, penalises \text{shortfall}:
scenarios in which the portfolio fails to cover the option obligation.
Financially, this is a solvency constraint.  A shortfall
$\xi_\tau > V_T^\theta$ means the hedger cannot meet the liability, which
constitutes a default event.  The second term, weighted by~$\lambda_o$,
penalises \text{over-hedging}: scenarios in which the portfolio exceeds
the obligation.  Financially, this is a capital efficiency constraint.  A
surplus $V_T^\theta > \xi_\tau$ means the hedger has immobilised more
capital than necessary, incurring an opportunity cost but without
threatening solvency.  The asymmetry $\lambda_s \gg \lambda_o$ reflects
the practical reality that default is far more costly than capital
inefficiency.

The third term targets the tail of the shortfall distribution using CVaR
rather than standard Value-at-Risk.  Standard VaR reports only the
threshold of a given quantile: it answers ``what is the worst loss at the
95th percentile?''\ but is blind to how bad losses can be beyond that
threshold.  CVaR averages over the entire tail beyond the quantile,
penalising the \text{severity} of the worst outcomes rather than merely
their \text{frequency} \citep{Artzner1999}.  For a hedger concerned with
catastrophic shortfall scenarios, CVaR is the appropriate measure because
it is sensitive to the shape of the tail, not just its location.

This loss does not enforce the strict domination $V_t \ge \xi_t$ for
all~$t$ that would characterise a full super-replicating strategy.
Instead, it encodes a \text{soft} preference for strategies whose
shortfall is small and whose tail risk is controlled.  From the perspective
of the optional decomposition, the connection is direct: the mean residual
hedging error of the trained model provides an empirical estimate of the
expected value of~$\tilde{C}$ (the average cost of incompleteness), while
the shortfall distribution characterises the pathwise behaviour
of~$\tilde{C}$ across scenarios (how the cost varies from one market
realisation to another).  The loss function therefore serves as the
training objective that drives the neural network toward the best possible
approximation of~$\tilde{\varphi}$, with the irreducible residual
revealing~$\tilde{C}$.  Calibration of the penalty parameters
$\lambda_s$, $\lambda_o$, and $\lambda_c$ is described in Chapter~4.

\paragraph{Path generation and robustness.}
Training requires large numbers of simulated price paths.  Alongside
standard parametric simulation from the GBM and Heston models, this thesis
employs a variational autoencoder (VAE) trained on historical multi-asset
equity data to generate synthetic trajectories.  The VAE serves as a
stress test for model misspecification: it captures empirical
distributional features --- volatility clustering, fat tails, and
cross-asset dependence structures --- that the parametric models may fail
to reproduce.  By evaluating hedging performance on both parametric and
VAE-generated paths, one can assess the extent to which conclusions depend
on the assumed data-generating process.  If a hedging strategy performs
well on parametric paths but poorly on VAE-generated paths, this signals
that the strategy has overfit to model-specific features rather than
learning a robust approximation of~$\tilde{\varphi}$.  The dataset and VAE
architecture are described in Chapter~3.

% 1.4  RESEARCH QUESTIONS
% =========================================================================
\subsection{Research Questions}
\label{subsec:research_questions}

The thesis addresses the following empirical research questions.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Approximation of the minimal dominating wealth process.}
  Can neural-network-based trading strategies, interpreted as estimators
  of the predictable integrand~$\tilde{\varphi}$, approximate the minimal
  dominating wealth process implied by martingale duality in multi-asset
  incomplete markets?  How closely do the deep BSDE solver, GRU network,
  and FNN with path signature features track the value process, and how
  do their residual errors compare under identical conditions?

\item \textbf{Structural versus dynamic incompleteness.}
  How does the source of market incompleteness affect the magnitude and
  distribution of the residual hedging error?  The multi-factor GBM model
  introduces incompleteness through a dimensional mismatch between traded
  assets and Brownian drivers (a structural feature fixed at model
  specification), while the Heston model introduces it through the random
  evolution of untraded variance processes (a dynamic feature that changes
  over time).  Comparing the two isolates the contribution of each
  mechanism to the total hedging cost.

\item \textbf{Recurrent memory and adapted cost components.}
  The cost process~$\tilde{C}$ in the optional decomposition is adapted
  but generally not predictable: its value at time~$t$ may depend on
  information revealed at~$t$ itself.  The GRU architecture maintains an
  evolving hidden state that is updated with each new observation,
  potentially capturing information that arrives simultaneously with the
  hedging decision.  The FNN with path signatures constructs a static,
  fixed-dimensional summary of the path history computed from data
  available strictly before the decision point.  Is the GRU's recurrent
  memory better suited to approximating the non-predictable, adapted
  component of the cost process~$\tilde{C}$ than the static signature
  encoding?

\item \textbf{Backward versus forward deep learning approaches.}
  The deep BSDE solver approximates the hedging problem through a
  backward equation whose solution directly
  encodes~$\tilde{\varphi}$.  The GRU and FNN-Sig architectures
  parameterise the strategy directly and optimise a forward risk
  functional.  How do these approaches compare in mean hedging error,
  tail risk (measured by CVaR), and computational cost?

\item \textbf{Robustness to model misspecification.}
  How do hedging models trained on VAE-generated paths, which reflect
  empirical distributional features learned from historical data, compare
  with those trained on parametric (GBM, Heston) simulations?  Does
  data-driven path generation, serving as a stress test for model
  assumptions, improve out-of-sample hedging performance?
\end{enumerate}

Each question is empirical.  The answers depend on measurable quantities
--- mean hedging error, shortfall probability, CVaR, and the distribution
of the residual cost --- estimated from simulated experiments with
controlled statistical uncertainty.

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{subsec:contributions}

The contributions of this thesis are computational and empirical.  All
mathematical results, pricing models, representation theorems, and
numerical solvers are drawn from the established literature.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Comparative evaluation under identical conditions.}
  A systematic empirical comparison of three neural-network-based hedging
  architectures --- the deep BSDE solver, a GRU recurrent network, and a
  feedforward network with path signature features --- applied to American
  put options under the same market models, loss function, and evaluation
  protocol.  A two-stage experimental design consisting of Bayesian
  hyperparameter optimisation \citep{Snoek2012} followed by retraining
  across multiple random seeds \citep{Henderson2018, Bouthillier2021}
  ensures that reported performance reflects model capability rather than
  fortunate initialisation.

\item \textbf{Two-model study isolating sources of incompleteness.}
  By comparing a multi-driver GBM model (structural incompleteness
  through dimensional mismatch) with a two-asset Heston model (dynamic
  incompleteness through stochastic volatility), the thesis disentangles
  the contributions of each mechanism to the residual hedging error and
  provides empirical evidence on how the \text{source} of
  incompleteness, not merely its presence, affects hedging difficulty.

\item \textbf{Tail-risk-based evaluation with actuarial rationale.}
  Performance is evaluated using the asymmetric loss function with a CVaR
  component, providing a risk-sensitive assessment that characterises the
  tail of the shortfall distribution.  The solvency-oriented framing of
  the shortfall penalty and the capital-efficiency interpretation of the
  over-hedging penalty align the evaluation with actuarial risk management
  principles.

\item \textbf{Empirical proxy for the cost of incompleteness.}
  The neural network output at each rebalancing date is interpreted as
  the estimator of the predictable integrand~$\tilde{\varphi}$, and the
  residual hedging error is interpreted as the empirical counterpart of
  the adapted cost process~$\tilde{C}$ in the optional decomposition.
  The thesis provides evidence consistent with the theoretical prediction
  that~$\tilde{C}$ is strictly positive in incomplete markets and
  approximately zero in the complete-market limit.

\item \textbf{VAE-based path generation as a stress test.}
  A variational autoencoder trained on historical multi-asset equity data
  provides a data-driven complement to parametric simulation.  By
  capturing volatility clustering, fat tails, and cross-asset dependence
  that parametric models may not reproduce, the VAE-generated paths serve
  as a stress test for model misspecification, enabling assessment of the
  robustness of hedging performance across data-generating assumptions.
\end{enumerate}

The thesis uses established methods as computational tools and evaluates
them on a problem --- multi-asset American options under controlled
incomplete-market models, interpreted through the optional decomposition
framework with explicit identification of the predictable and adapted
components --- that has not been studied in this combination of models,
instruments, architectures, and interpretive lens.
